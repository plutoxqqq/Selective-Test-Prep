# Expansion and Simplification of Perfect Squares

## Part 1: Identifying

A perfect square looks like this: $(a+b)^2$. And it can be expanded to this: $a^2+2ab+b^2$. Do not get it confused with $(a \cdot b)^2$ because $(a+b)^2$ is not $a^2 + b^2$

## Part 2: Expanding

### Positives $(a+b)^2$
When expanding a positive perfect square, you just substitute in the values per the formula $a^2+2ab+b^2$. For example:

Expand $(x+5)^2$:

**Step 1 - Square the First Term**

$x^2$

**Step 2 - Double the Product of A and B**

$2(x)(5) = 10x$

**Step 3 - Square the Last Term**

$5^2 = 25$

**Answer**

$x^2 + 10x + 25$

<br>
<br>

### Negatives $(a-b)^2$
When expanding a negative perfect square, you do the same as with a positive one and substitute in the values. However, there is one small difference, the formula now becomes $a^2-2ab+b^2$. Notice the first operation is now a minus, but the second is still a plus. Example:

Expand $(x-5)^2$:

**Step 1 - Square the First Term**

$x^2$

**Step 2 - Double the Product of A and B**

$2(x)(5) = 10x$

**Step 3 - Square the Last Term**

$5^2 = 25$

**Answer**

$x^2 - 10x + 25$
